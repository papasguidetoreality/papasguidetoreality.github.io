Immigrants Wanted

The United States at its inception was large and underpopulated, and its founders knew that millions of immigrants would be needed to develop, defend and ultimately expand it. In the early 1800s, though, the inflows of immigrants and the need for settlers in new, western territories created strong political pressures for expanded enfranchisement. By the time of the Civil War, most adult white males in the US could vote without restriction.

American politicians routinely took advantage of these electorate expansions by buying, in one way or another, votes that new and impecunious voters were only too happy to sell. But at a time when ‚Äústates‚Äô rights‚Äù still meant something, the effects were mostly local; and in general, electorate expansion in the pre-Civil War era was something that almost every faction supported and exploited.

African-Americans and Women

The Civil War changed this situation dramatically. President Lincoln initially felt that African Americans comprised a distinct population that, after emancipation, should live and govern themselves according to their own ways, on their own lands. However, Congress was unenthusiastic about bearing the costs of moving this large population. Ultimately, as the war ground on, the Republicans decided on full citizenship for African Americans, who could thus serve in the Union army, and‚Äîmore importantly‚Äîcould become critical counterweights in the South to bitter, Democrat-voting Whites. The partisan motivation for this policy was concealed by grand moral sentiments, establishing the pattern that would be followed in future electorate expansions.

As Black males became loyal Republican voters throughout the Reconstruction South,[1] the many White women who had supported Black suffrage now turned their energies toward their own. Here again, the Republicans saw an opportunity for electoral payoffs, ultimately providing nearly three-quarters of the ‚Äúyea‚Äù votes in Congress for the passage of the 19th Amendment, which outlawed voting rights restrictions based on sex.

A Reversal of Fortune

Within a few decades, the Democrats began reversing these Republican advantages with a vengeance. They captured the Black vote by remaking themselves as the party of Black civil rights and integration, and kept Blacks on side with generous welfare and affirmative-action legislation. By the 1968 election, Black neighborhoods were voting Democrat at rates approaching 100 percent. The Democrats‚Äô civil rights and ‚Äúwomen‚Äôs liberation‚Äù policies also increasingly endeared them to women, especially younger women, opening up a gender gap in the party‚Äôs favor that would soon widen dramatically.

In 1972, the Democrat-controlled Congress approved a constitutional amendment that further expanded the electorate by lowering the voting age from 21 to 18. Again, these partisan measures were sold to the country as lofty moral achievements.

The main response of the outmaneuvered GOP, in the Reagan and Bush administrations, was to try to improve their image among immigrants by signing on to various immigration-liberalizing bills, including amnesties for illegals. It availed them little, as the Democrats tightened their grip on every group that had become voters through immigration and other electorate expansions. In the 2012 presidential election,[2] the Democratic candidate received 93 percent of the Black vote, 71 percent of the Hispanic vote, 73 percent of the Asian vote, 85 percent of the Muslim vote, 55 percent of the female vote, 63 percent of the low-household-income (<$30K) vote, and 60 percent of the vote among 18-to-29-year-olds. Endgame

Have nearly 200 years of electorate-packing, lately with tens of millions of people from developing countries, made a travesty of democracy and ruined the U.S. as a nation? Republicans may think so. But as they bewail this situation, they should at least acknowledge their own large part in creating it.
